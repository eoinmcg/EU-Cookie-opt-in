EU Cookie Law

Practical guide for website owners

Cookies. A word most people associate with chocalate chips and coffee breaks has a rather different meaning on the web. In computer jargon, a cookie is a small piece of information that a website stores and then reads and modifies on your computer. Cookies cannot send viruses or install nasty things on your machine. They were designed for the benign purpose of remebering things such as logging in, your preferences or the contents. of your shopping cart. Depending on your surfing habits you have anywhere between hundreds and thousands of theses innocous little critters stored on your computer. This type of cookie is commonly known as a first party cookie.

The flip side of these small digital helpers is that they be put to work for shadier purposes. A common tactic employed by advertisers, not to mention Facebook's ubiquitous like button, is to to track users across all sites on which they appear. This allows a very comprehensive picture of your online habits to stitched together and is a gold mine when it comes to targetted advertising and marketing intelligence. Such cookies are referred to as third party cookies. For many, it also represents an violation of privacy.

As part of the EU's Citizens Rights Directive, which comes into force on 26th May 2012, website service provider's must gain the user's consent before dropping cookies on their computer. Failure to do so could result in fines of up to £500,000.

What is entails

According to the law you must gain user's consent. The most obvious way to do this is with a message appearing to all visitors to your site, giving them the option to accept, and continue browsing, or reject and leave your site. Sites that require registration could explain how they use cookies at the sign up stage, hence gaining permission.

How does it effect me

The most immediate concern is that visitors are frightened away by message a popup asking them to accept your cookies. Most visitors probably don't know what a cookie is. This could potentially lead to loss of business or sales.

If your site only uses cookies via google analytics you could always drop analytics, but at the cost of loosing a valuable insight into website performance. The exact kind of intelligence needed to succeed in today's ultra competitive market place.

What can I do about it

Broadly speaking, a website owner has two choices;

  1. Follow the law to the letter and notify all users that your site uses cookies, giving them the option to accept and be tracked or to decline and browse away from your site. Click here to see a demo of a solution we have implemented for our clients.

  2. Wait and see how rigoursly the law will be enforced. Most websites use some form of traffic analyisis, such as google analytics, to paint a broad picture of website performance.
